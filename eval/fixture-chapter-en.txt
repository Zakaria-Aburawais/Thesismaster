# Chapter 3 — Methodology and Data Governance

This chapter sets out the mixed-methods design used to examine adaptive governance in federated energy systems. The design follows the sequential explanatory model described by Almasi and Verren, in which a quantitative survey phase establishes broad patterns and a qualitative interview phase interrogates the mechanisms behind them [1]. The survey instrument was adapted from the Fictional Governance Index and translated into three working languages before piloting.

The sampling frame deserves particular attention because it shapes every claim made in Chapters 4 and 5. Municipal utilities were stratified by size, ownership model, and regulatory regime, and a proportional random sample was drawn within each stratum. The measurement campaign was repeated across three independent metropolitan sites to control for regional policy effects, and enumerators followed a common written protocol at every stage of the fieldwork so that procedural drift between teams could not contaminate the comparison.

Prior studies of distributed infrastructure have documented persistent gaps between formal decision rights and operational practice [2-4]. The present design responds to that literature in two ways. First, decision rights were coded from statutes and bylaws rather than from self-report. Second, operational practice was observed directly during scheduled control-room visits (Almasi, 2021).

One reviewer of the pilot instrument observed that “the questionnaire measures what managers say about coordination, not what their dispatch logs actually record about coordination” — a criticism the final design addresses by pairing every self-report item with a log-derived behavioural indicator.

A second methodological caution comes from Verren, who argues that “grid federation studies systematically overstate institutional convergence because they sample only utilities that survived the reform period” [5]. Survivorship correction weights were therefore applied to all cross-sectional estimates.

The interview phase used a semi-structured protocol with fourteen prompts, each mapped to a construct in the analytical framework. Interviews averaged fifty-one minutes and were transcribed verbatim. Coding proceeded in two cycles: a descriptive first cycle using the a-priori codebook, and a pattern-coding second cycle in which emergent categories were permitted. Intercoder reliability reached a Cohen's kappa of 0.81 on the held-out reliability sample, which exceeds the conventional threshold for substantive analysis. The measurement campaign was repeated across three independent metropolitan sites to control for seasonal load variation as well.

Ethical clearance was granted by the Fictional University Research Board under protocol FURB-2020-117. All participants received an information sheet and signed consent forms; utilities are identified only by stratum codes throughout. Data handling follows the retention schedule agreed with the board [7,9].

The quantitative and qualitative strands were integrated through a joint display matrix in which each survey construct is confronted with its interview-derived counterpart. Divergent findings were not smoothed over; they are reported as tensions and examined case by case in Chapter 5. This treatment of divergence follows the tradition of analytical transparency advocated in recent methods scholarship [6].
